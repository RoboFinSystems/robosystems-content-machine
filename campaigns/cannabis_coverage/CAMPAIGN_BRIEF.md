# Campaign: RoboSystems Initiating Coverage — Cannabis Industry

## Campaign Thesis

The US cannabis industry is a black hole for institutional coverage. Because cannabis remains a Schedule I substance, US multi-state operators (MSOs) are banished to OTC markets. No major exchange listings. No sell-side coverage. No institutional ownership. No analyst reports. For an industry generating billions in revenue, it is the most under-covered sector in US public markets.

This is both the problem and the opportunity.

RoboSystems is initiating coverage on the cannabis industry by doing what nobody else can at this scale: pulling real XBRL financial data from SEC filings for every major MSO, running structured analysis, and producing narrated video breakdowns of each company. Every video fills a vacuum that Wall Street has chosen to ignore.

We are building what should already exist but doesn't: a library of detailed, fact-based company reports for every major MSO, grounded in real SEC filing data. Not buy/sell recommendations — but not sterile either. The catalysts facing this industry are real and worth understanding. Our job is to show where each company stands today, how the math changes under each catalyst scenario, and what the risks are. Strengths and weaknesses with equal specificity. Let the audience decide what to do with it.

## Macro Context

### The Bust Period (2022-Present)

The cannabis industry went through a massive boom-bust cycle:

- **2020-2021 boom**: Biden election created expectations of federal legalization. Capital flooded in. MSOs raised billions, acquired aggressively, and built out cultivation/retail at peak valuations.
- **2022-present bust**: Legalization stalled. 280E tax burden crushed profitability. Wholesale prices collapsed. Goodwill impairments wiped out billions. Stock prices fell 70-90% from peaks.
- **Current state (March 2026)**: Industry is in a scarce capital period. Many operators are cash-flow positive but trade at deep discounts to book value. National cannabis sales in 2025 were slightly lower than 2024 due to pricing pressure and oversupply compressing margins for cultivators and retailers.

**State-level dynamics driving the bust:**
- **Illinois** (3rd-largest US market): First-ever drop in annual recreational revenue — 2024 saw $1.7B, but 2025 fell ~13% to $1.5B. Prices remain elevated ($257/oz) vs neighboring states, creating cross-border leakage pressure.
- **Florida**: Recreational ballot question failed in Nov 2024. Operators who had built inventory and expanded stores for anticipated adult-use demand were forced to heavily discount. This was a significant capital destruction event for FL-heavy operators (especially Trulieve).
- **New Jersey**: Climbing ~5% in pricing but also saw its largest spot index loss on record recently (14.2% drop per pound).
- **New York, Ohio, Minnesota**: Newer markets posting 100%+ gains for 2025, providing growth for operators with exposure. But these markets are early-stage and not yet generating meaningful profit.
- **California, Oregon, Colorado**: Mature oversupplied markets with severe price compression. Most MSOs have limited exposure here.

### The Catalyst Stack

Multiple catalysts are converging simultaneously — all uncertain, all material:

#### 1. Rescheduling (Schedule I → Schedule III)

**Timeline and current status (as of March 2026):**
- **Aug 2023**: HHS recommended to DEA that marijuana be transferred from Schedule I to Schedule III based on scientific and medical evaluation.
- **May 2024**: DEA proposed a rule to reschedule marijuana to Schedule III (Notice of Proposed Rulemaking).
- **Dec 2, 2024**: DEA administrative hearing proceedings began. Judge Mulrooney issued a pre-hearing ruling setting a schedule from Jan 21 to Mar 6 for substantive testimony.
- **Jan 2025**: Chief Judge Mulrooney canceled the scheduled hearing and suspended proceedings pending resolution of an appeal. This was a major setback.
- **Dec 18, 2025**: Trump signed executive order "Increasing Medical Marijuana and Cannabidiol Research" directing the AG to "take all necessary steps to complete the rulemaking process related to rescheduling marijuana to Schedule III of the CSA in the most expeditious manner."
- **March 2026 (now)**: Cannabis STILL remains Schedule I. The EO does not reschedule marijuana — it instructs DOJ/DEA to finish the rulemaking. The DEA has no judge, the AG has gone silent, and 48 GOP lawmakers have publicly opposed rescheduling.

**What rescheduling would enable:**
- Elimination of Section 280E (see below)
- Potential uplisting to NYSE/NASDAQ — though the exchanges have previously indicated that Schedule III alone may not be sufficient to permit listing of plant-touching companies. Whether they revisit that position post-rescheduling remains uncertain.
- Improved access to banking and financial services (reduced compliance concerns)
- Institutional capital access (most funds can't touch Schedule I companies)
- Accelerated research — universities and pharmaceutical companies could study cannabis

**Key risk**: Completion depends on how DEA manages the record and whether it reopens or supplements procedures. Stakeholders should plan for multiple scenarios, including a final rule in 2026 AND alternatives where litigation delays implementation further.

**Emerging signal (March 2026)**: There are credible rumors that the final rescheduling rule could drop imminently, potentially accompanied by a "Bondi memo" — AG Pam Bondi issuing DOJ enforcement guidance analogous to the Cole Memorandum from the Obama era (2013). The Cole memo effectively told US Attorneys to stand down on prosecuting state-legal cannabis businesses, providing the de facto federal cover that enabled the state-legal industry to grow. Sessions rescinded it in 2018. A Bondi memo could go further:
- **Tax guidance**: Formal DOJ/IRS guidance on 280E applicability and treatment during the rescheduling transition
- **Interstate commerce**: Potentially the most transformative element — guidance permitting or deprioritizing enforcement against interstate cannabis commerce between legal states. This would fundamentally reshape the MSO model, enabling national supply chains, cross-state brand distribution, and eliminating the inefficiency of state-by-state vertical integration.

If the Bondi memo addresses interstate commerce, the implications cascade rapidly: MSOs with multi-state cultivation and retail infrastructure become national consumer brands overnight. Companies with excess cultivation capacity in low-cost states gain structural advantage. The consolidation thesis accelerates as national scale becomes possible.

#### 2. 280E Tax Relief

Section 280E of the Internal Revenue Code prohibits cannabis companies from deducting ordinary and necessary business expenses (rent, salaries, marketing, utilities) because they traffic in a Schedule I or II controlled substance. Only cost of goods sold (COGS) is deductible. This means companies are taxed on gross income rather than net income.

**The math is devastating:**
- Effective tax rates for cannabis companies routinely exceed 70% (vs 21% federal corporate rate)
- Example: Green Thumb Industries paid $147M in income taxes on $263M pretax income in FY2025 — a 56% effective rate. A normal company would have paid ~$55M. The 280E penalty: ~$92M/year in excess tax.
- Some companies show profit before tax but massive losses after tax, making GAAP net income nearly meaningless for comparing cannabis companies to normal businesses.

**Rescheduling impact on 280E:**
- Rescheduling to Schedule III would render Section 280E inapplicable. Companies could deduct business expenses like any other business.
- Relief will almost certainly be **prospective** — effective once DEA's final rule becomes official (expected first half of 2026 if process continues).
- **Retroactive relief is unlikely**: IRS has explicitly stated that amended return claims filed in anticipation of rescheduling "lack merit." However, companies can file "protective refund claims" for open tax years (within 3 years of filing) to preserve their right to claim retroactive relief if Congress or IRS provides favorable guidance later.
- Companies with existing IRS tax debt may be able to pursue discounted settlements under the IRS Offer in Compromise program post-rescheduling.

**Important legal challenge**: New Mexico Top Organics, Inc. v. Commissioner (Tax Court Docket #19661-24, filed Dec 2024) represents one of the first major legal challenges to 280E's applicability.

#### 3. Capital Scarcity Moat

Because cannabis is federally illegal, traditional capital markets are closed. This creates a paradoxical advantage for survivors:
- Companies that made it through the bust with assets intact have a structural moat — it will take years and major regulatory changes for new capital to enter and build competing infrastructure
- Existing licenses, cultivation facilities, and retail locations are extremely difficult to replicate (especially in limited-license states)
- MSO debt is expensive (9-14% interest rates are common) because traditional lenders won't touch the sector. Companies that have managed to refinance and extend maturities are in stronger positions.

#### 4. Consolidation Wave

**Current reality (tempered expectations):**
Each year brings predictions of a major M&A wave in cannabis, but each year mostly delivers tuck-in deals rather than transformative consolidation. Investors should expect a slow buildup rather than a speculative moonshot in 2026, with fundamentals gradually improving as conditions become more welcoming.

**Consolidation drivers:**
- MSO-to-MSO: Stronger operators acquiring weaker ones at distressed valuations. The industry is in the "Scale" stage — larger players capture market share as smaller ones drop off.
- Outside industries: Tobacco (Philip Morris), alcohol (Constellation Brands/Canopy), and CPG companies making strategic moves. The wider CPG industry faces slowed growth and sees cannabis normalization as a growth vector.
- Strategic acquirers, private equity, family offices, and corporate investors previously constrained by federal uncertainty are increasingly active.
- Canadian LPs may serve as consolidation vehicles for US listings on major exchanges.

**Post-rescheduling acceleration**: Improved cash flow, normalized taxation, and capital inflows are expected to accelerate M&A across the cannabis value chain.

#### 5. Secular Consumer Shift

**Alcohol → Cannabis trend:**
- Structural shift particularly among younger demographics away from alcohol toward cannabis.
- THC beverage sales hit $1.0-$1.3B in 2024, with potential to grow to $9.9-$14.9B if even a small share of alcohol consumers switch.
- Most THC beverages are microdosed (2-5mg), making them approachable for newcomers.
- Major alcohol distributors are already exploring hemp-derived THC beverages.

**CRITICAL REGULATORY RISK — Hemp crackdown:**
Beginning November 13, 2026, hemp products can only be sold if they contain no more than 0.4mg of THC per container. This move would wipe out ~95% of products from the $28.4 billion hemp industry. This is both a threat (if MSOs have hemp exposure) and an opportunity (eliminates low-cost hemp-derived competition to dispensary products).

#### 6. Medicare CBD Reimbursement

Trump's Dec 2025 executive order established a CMS Innovation Center model:
- Medicare beneficiaries can receive **up to $500 annually in hemp-derived CBD products** when recommended by a physician
- Pilot program set to begin **April 2026**
- CMS required to collect and publicly report outcome data on beneficiaries
- Order also directs NIDA to accelerate research on cannabis/cannabinoid benefits and risks
- Urges Congress to clarify the federal definition of hemp-derived cannabinoids

This could create a massive new revenue channel for operators with pharmaceutical-grade production capabilities, though the initial pilot is small in scope.

All catalysts are UNCERTAIN. The market is pricing in the probability-weighted downside:
- Rescheduling could stall indefinitely — DEA has no judge, AG is silent, 48 GOP lawmakers oppose
- 280E relief will NOT be retroactive per IRS
- NYSE/NASDAQ have indicated Schedule III alone may not enable uplisting
- State-level oversupply and price compression continue in mature markets
- The Nov 2026 hemp product restriction could disrupt adjacent revenue streams
- Florida recreational failure hurt operators who had expanded for anticipated demand

This uncertainty is exactly why there's no coverage. And it's exactly why coverage is valuable.

The catalysts are real — rescheduling is in process, the executive order exists, 280E relief would materially change these companies' financials. But so are the risks — rescheduling has stalled before, the DEA process is uncertain, and state-level headwinds continue. We present both sides with specificity and let the audience draw their own conclusions.

## Campaign Strategy

### Framing
Every video should:
1. **Lead with the facts** — what does the actual SEC filing tell us about this company? Revenue, margins, cash flow, debt, tax burden. No opinions — numbers.
2. **Contextualize within the sector** — how does this company compare to peers? Rank it on the metrics that matter.
3. **Map the catalyst scenarios** — build pro-forma views: 280E-adjusted earnings, interstate commerce positioning, consolidation math. Show how the numbers change under each scenario. Let the viewer see the "before and after."
4. **Identify structural advantages** — not all MSOs are equal. Which companies have the balance sheet, the geographic footprint, the margins, and the operational discipline to take advantage of catalysts as they progress?
5. **Be honest about risks** — present the bear case as concretely as the bull case. Specific risks, not generic disclaimers.
6. **Don't tell people what to do** — give them the facts and the framework. They decide.
7. **Fill the vacuum** — explicitly position these videos as "the analysis that doesn't exist elsewhere"

## Key Financial Metrics to Analyze

Cannabis-specific metrics that matter beyond standard analysis:

| Metric | Why It Matters |
|--------|---------------|
| **Effective tax rate** | 280E creates 50-80%+ effective rates. This is THE metric for understanding the 280E burden. Compare: GTBIF at 56%, normal company at 21%. |
| **Income tax expense vs pretax income** | Shows the 280E distortion. Some companies show profit before tax but massive losses after. |
| **Goodwill & intangible asset impairments** | Boom-era acquisitions being written down. Shows how much value destruction has occurred. |
| **Operating cash flow vs net income** | Cash flow often tells a different story than GAAP earnings due to 280E and impairments. TCNNF: record $273M OCF in FY2025 despite $116M net loss. |
| **Gross margin** | Vertical integration benefits. TCNNF achieved 60% gross margin in FY2025. CURLF at 50% adjusted. |
| **Debt maturity schedule** | Who needs to refinance? In a scarce capital market with 9-14% rates, this determines survival. TCNNF redeemed $368M of 2026 notes and issued $140M of 2030 notes. |
| **Revenue per state/market** | Geographic concentration risk. FL-heavy operators got hurt by the ballot failure. IL operators facing 13% revenue decline. NY/OH operators seeing growth. |
| **SG&A as % of revenue** | Operating leverage — are they getting more efficient or bloated from the boom? |
| **CapEx trends** | Are they still investing or in harvest mode? Scarce capital = harvest mode for most. |
| **Retail vs wholesale mix** | Retail revenue (dispensaries) tends to be higher margin. GTBIF: 71% retail, 29% CPG. |
| **Free cash flow** | The ultimate health metric. TCNNF: $229M FCF. GTBIF: positive but smaller. |
