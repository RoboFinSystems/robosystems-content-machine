# The 280E Scorecard: Nine U.S. Cannabis Operators, One Tax Code

**A cross-company analysis of the largest pure-play U.S. multi-state operators — FY2025, built from SEC XBRL filings through the RoboSystems knowledge graph.**

---

In fiscal 2025, the nine largest pure-play U.S. cannabis operators generated **$4.7 billion in revenue** and **$2.3 billion in gross profit** — a blended gross margin near 50%, the profile of a healthy consumer-products industry.

They posted a **combined pre-tax loss of roughly $69 million.**

And they still owed **$606 million in income tax.**

Eight of the nine paid income tax while losing money. The one company that turned a GAAP profit — Green Thumb Industries — paid **$147 million in tax, more than its entire $138 million of operating income.** This is not a story about nine badly run companies. Eight of the nine ran positive operating cash flow despite the GAAP losses. It is a story about one line of the U.S. tax code, **Section 280E**, and what it does to a margin-healthy industry that the federal government still classifies alongside heroin.

Every figure in the tables below was pulled live from each company's SEC XBRL filings through the RoboSystems knowledge graph. This is what cross-company financial analysis looks like when the filings are a queryable database instead of a stack of PDFs.

> **What 280E does.** Section 280E bars any business "trafficking" a Schedule I substance from deducting ordinary operating expenses — rent, payroll, marketing, interest. Only cost of goods sold is deductible. The result: cannabis operators are taxed on **gross profit, not net income**, which produces large tax bills on top of pre-tax losses. A normal company pays ~21% of *pre-tax income*. As the scorecard shows, most of these operators paid **22%–31% of gross profit** — whether or not they made money.

## The methodology, briefly

- **Graph-sourced (SEC XBRL, FY2025, period ending 12/31/2025):** revenue, gross profit, operating income, income-tax expense, net income, cash, total assets, goodwill, stockholders' equity, and unrecognized tax benefits. Pulled directly from each filer's 10-K via the RoboSystems graph. The combined pre-tax figure is net income plus income-tax expense — an approximation that does not adjust for noncontrolling interests or discontinued operations (e.g., TerrAscend's Michigan exit).
- **Filing/management-reported:** adjusted EBITDA (a non-GAAP measure), net debt including leases, debt-maturity schedules, and the medical-vs-adult-use revenue mix.
- **Scenario ranges** reflect a re-rating toward consumer-staples multiples (CPG ~10–14×, Altria ~8–9×, Constellation ~12×) if 280E is lifted. They are **not price targets and not advice.**

<sub>³ Unrecognized tax benefits (UTB): disputed taxes the operators have booked as reserves while contesting that 280E applies — i.e., taxes claimed but not paid, pending litigation. This is the heart of it. **A normal corporation pays roughly 21% of pre-tax income.** These nine paid **26% of gross profit** — and the sector had no pre-tax income to speak of. The $606M tax bill landed on a combined **pre-tax loss of ~$69M.**

Two things the graph surfaces that the headlines miss:

- **The reserves are enormous and nearly universal.** The nine carry **~$2.1 billion of unrecognized tax benefits** — disputed 280E taxes they've booked but are fighting in court rather than paying. For the smaller names, the reserve *exceeds the entire market cap*. This is the industry's collective bet that 280E will ultimately be ruled inapplicable; the IRS is litigating the other side (see Risks). The gap is visible in cash flow, too: the nine booked $606M of income-tax *expense* in FY2025 but paid only **~$127M of it in cash** — the ~$479M difference is 280E they're reserving rather than remitting (Verano, paying $78M of its $92M, is the exception).
- **Green Thumb is not the exception people think.** It carries a $203M reserve, in line with Ascend. The thing that sets Green Thumb apart isn't restraint on the tax fight — it's that it is **profitable enough to absorb the tax at all.** Everyone is contesting 280E. Only Green Thumb is winning the operating business underneath it.

## The catalyst: a tax switch, half-flipped

The entire sector is a leveraged bet on one variable — whether, and how broadly, cannabis moves off Schedule I. As of mid-2026 the switch is **half-flipped**, and that asymmetry is the whole investable distinction between these nine.

- **Stage 1 — DONE (April 2026):** A partial order moved **state-licensed *medical* marijuana to Schedule III**, lifting 280E on medical income only, prospectively for the full 2026 tax year. Adult-use stays Schedule I. The first visible benefit shows up in Q2 FY2026 results (Q1 closed before the effective date).
- **Stage 2 — CONTESTED (now):** A DEA hearing on moving **all** marijuana to Schedule III — the bigger prize that lifts 280E from adult-use too — is underway and widely described as stacked with opponents. A parallel D.C. Circuit emergency stay could even vacate the medical relief.

## The tiers

**Tier 1 — The Survivors.** *Green Thumb (GTBIF), Trulieve (TRLV).* Net cash, real GAAP economics, the cleanest balance sheets. Green Thumb is the only consistently profitable major and a buyer, not a target; its catalyst (adult-use relief) simply hasn't arrived. Trulieve is the cleanest expression of the *near-term* trade — 60% margins, medical-heavy, already restructured to capture the medical fix, and the first plant-touching operator on the NYSE. They win on either timeline.

## The risks that bind the whole group

- **The IRS is litigating the gambit.** That ~$2.1B of unrecognized tax benefits assumes the operators' position prevails. The IRS sued **TerrAscend in May 2026 to claw back an $8.3M refund** — the first known 280E clawback — and is examining multiple filers. If the position fails, the reserves become real liabilities.
- **The broad order may not come.** The DEA hearing is contested; a D.C. Circuit stay could even unwind the medical relief. Stage 2 is optionality, not a base case.
- **A nine-MSO antitrust overhang.** A February 2026 state "cannabis cartel" price-fixing suit names nine operators — a live overhang for the group.
- **Impairment and dilution scars.** Several names carry boom-cycle goodwill/intangible write-downs; the roll-ups (Vireo) dilute relentlessly.

---

## Bottom line

Strip out one line of the tax code and these nine companies earn roughly **$2.3 billion of gross profit a year** at consumer-staples margins. Leave it in, and the same nine post a collective net loss and carry $2.1 billion of disputed taxes on their balance sheets. That gap — between the operating business and the after-tax business — *is* the cannabis trade. **Tier 1 owns the business; Tier 2 owns the operating leverage to the tax; Tier 3 owns the option on the tax itself.** Which one you want depends entirely on your read of a switch that, as of today, is half-flipped and being fought over in two federal venues at once.
