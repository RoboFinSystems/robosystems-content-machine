The Financial Knowledge Graph Manifesto

Why the future of business intelligence lies not in isolated metrics, but in the rich, interconnected relationships between financial outcomes and the operations that drive them.

JF
Joey French
Sep 1, 2025 · 6 min read

Every financial system is built on a lie

The lie is this: that rows and columns can capture the reality of business. Think about your last invoice. In your accounting system, it's row #47291 in a transactions table. But that invoice is actually the intersection of a dozen stories — a salesperson closing a deal, a warehouse shipping product, a customer solving a problem.

We force these living relationships into rigid tables, then wonder why our financial insights feel dead on arrival. This isn't a technology problem. It's a philosophy problem.

Money doesn't move in tables. It flows through networks.

The intelligence hidden in connections

Traditional BI asks: "What happened?" Knowledge graphs reveal: "Why did it happen, and what happens next?" When you model finance as a graph rather than tables, the complexity doesn't disappear — it becomes navigable. One query traverses the entire network, calculating true unit profitability in milliseconds, not months.
